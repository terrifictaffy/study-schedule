Chapter 8 — Confidence Intervals 2 hrs

  • CI interpretation: "95% confident the true parameter lies in this interval" — NOT "95% chance μ is here"
  • CI for proportion: p̂ ± z*(√(p̂(1−p̂)/n)) — check conditions: Random, Normal (np ≥ 10 & n(1−p̂) ≥ 10), Independent
  • CI for mean (σ unknown): x̄ ± t*(s/√n) — t-distribution with df = n−1
  • Find z* and t* for 90%, 95%, 99% confidence from tables
  • Margin of error = z* or t* × (SE); wider CI = higher confidence or smaller n
  • PANIC: Parameter → Assumptions → Name the interval → Interval (calculate) → Conclude in context

Bioenergetics, Cofactors & Enzyme Classes 2 hrs · Ch 15 Review

  • 🃏 Quizlet first: Run bioenergetics & cofactor deck to activate memory before notes
  • Keq ↔ ΔG°′: ΔG°′ = −RT ln Keq; large Keq = spontaneous = negative ΔG°′
  • ΔG′ = ΔG°′ + RT ln([products]/[reactants]) — actual free energy change; know how concentration shifts equilibrium position away from standard conditions
  • ATP structure: adenine + ribose + 3 phosphates; γ-phosphate has high transfer potential because of electrostatic repulsion in ATP and resonance/solvation stabilization of ADP + Pi products
  • Phosphoryl group transfer potential — Table 15.1: PEP > 1,3-BPG > ATP > G6P. Compounds above ATP can drive ATP synthesis; below ATP, ATP drives their phosphorylation
  • Kinases: transfer phosphoryl from ATP to substrate. Phosphatases: hydrolyze phosphate off. Identify both in glycolysis steps
  • Cofactors: NAD⁺/NADH = 2e⁻ + H⁺ carrier (soluble, used in dehydrogenase reactions); FAD/FADH₂ = 2e⁻ carrier (bound to enzyme); Coenzyme A = acyl group carrier via thioester bond
  • Energy charge = [ATP + ½ADP] / [ATP + ADP + AMP] — high charge (~1.0) shuts down catabolism; low charge stimulates it
  • 6 enzyme classes to know by name and example: Oxido-reductases (dehydrogenases, step 6 GAPDH), Transferases (kinases, step 1 hexokinase), Hydrolases, Lyases (aldolase step 4, enolase step 9), Isomerases (TPI step 5), Ligases
  • Cellular locations: Glycolysis (cytosol), PDHC & TCA (mitochondrial matrix), ETC (inner mitochondrial membrane), PPP (cytosol), Glycogen synthesis/breakdown (cytosol), Glucose-6-phosphatase (ER membrane — liver only)
  • 📖 Workbook Self-Test Ch 15 problems 11–13, 15–18, 20 + Textbook problems 7, 9: apply ΔG math without notes

Glycolysis — Steps, Fisher Projections & 3 Key Mechanisms 3 hrs · Ch 16

  • 🃏 Quizlet: Run glycolysis step deck — substrate, product, enzyme, cofactor for each step
  • Logic: understand the rationale for the ORDER and TYPE of each reaction (Table 16.1 & Fig 16.2) — don't just memorize, understand why
  • Stages 1 & 2 (energy investment, steps 1–5): Fischer projections of linear structures for all reactants & products; cyclic structures of stage 1 metabolites (glucose, G6P, F6P, F1,6BP)
  • Stage 3 (energy recovery, steps 6–10): 4 ATP + 2 NADH produced per glucose entering here; net 2 ATP for full glycolysis
  • Chemical transformations at each step: type of reaction (phosphorylation, isomerization, oxidation, etc.)
  • 3 enzyme mechanisms — know nucleophile, electrophile, electron flow, type of catalysis:
    • Step 1 — Hexokinase (Phosphoryl Transferase): C6-OH of glucose is nucleophile; attacks electrophilic γ-phosphorus of ATP; Mg²⁺ coordinates phosphates; SN2-like inline displacement
    • Step 6 — GAPDH (oxidation coupled to phosphorylation): Cys-SH attacks aldehyde C1 → thiohemiacetal → NAD⁺ oxidizes → thioester intermediate → inorganic Pi attacks thioester → releases 1,3-BPG; "oxidation coupled to phosphorylation" = one step does both
    • Step 10 — Pyruvate Kinase (Substrate Level Phosphorylation, SLP): PEP (high phosphoryl transfer potential) donates phosphoryl to ADP → pyruvate + ATP; SLP means ATP is made directly from a substrate, not via ETC proton gradient
  • Any cofactors or small molecules at each step — NAD⁺ reduced to NADH (step 6), ATP consumed (steps 1, 3), ATP produced (steps 7, 10), Pi consumed (step 6)
  • 📖 Workbook Self-Test Ch 16 problems 16–30 + Textbook problems 7, 10, 12, 18, 20, 21: mechanism & reaction type questions without notes

Pyruvate Fates, PDHC, TCA, ETC & Full ATP Accounting 3 hrs · Ch 16

  • 🃏 Quizlet: Pyruvate fates + aerobic/anaerobic energy yield deck
  • 3 fates of pyruvate: (1) Aerobic → Acetyl-CoA (PDHC); (2) Anaerobic → Lactate (LDH, regenerates NAD⁺); (3) Anaerobic fermentation → Ethanol + CO₂ (pyruvate decarboxylase + ADH, yeast)
  • Anaerobic fermentation purpose: regenerate NAD⁺ from NADH to maintain redox balance so glycolysis can continue (cytosol has no other NAD⁺ regeneration method without O₂)
  • PDHC (mitochondrial matrix): pyruvate + CoA + NAD⁺ → Acetyl-CoA + CO₂ + NADH; 5 cofactors: TPP, lipoic acid, CoA, FAD, NAD⁺; irreversible — commits carbon to aerobic fate
  • Fates of Glucose-6-Phosphate: glycolysis (energy production), glycogen synthesis (storage), or PPP (NADPH + ribose-5-P)
  • Fates of Acetyl-CoA: TCA cycle (oxidation → NADH/FADH₂), fatty acid synthesis (anabolism), ketone body synthesis (liver, fasting)
  • Citric Acid Cycle (8 steps, matrix): 1 Acetyl-CoA → 2 CO₂ + 3 NADH + 1 FADH₂ + 1 GTP per turn; entry via citrate synthase; key regulation: isocitrate DH & α-KG DH
  • ETC (inner mitochondrial membrane): NADH → Complex I → Ubiquinone (Q) → Complex III → Cyt c → Complex IV → O₂; FADH₂ → Complex II → Q; electron flow pumps H⁺ into intermembrane space
  • Oxidative Phosphorylation: proton gradient drives ATP synthase (F₀F₁); ~2.5 ATP per NADH, ~1.5 ATP per FADH₂
  • Anaerobic vs. Aerobic ATP — number of ATP/NADH produced: glycolysis (2 net ATP + 2 NADH), PDHC (2 NADH), TCA (6 NADH + 2 FADH₂ + 2 GTP per glucose) → aerobic total ~30–32 ATP; anaerobic = 2 ATP only
  • Phosphoryl Group Transfer Reactions and SLP steps in glycolysis & GNG that involve energy transformations — identify each one
  • 📝 Student Companion problems 5–19, 22, 23, 34–41, 46, 48, 50: full exam simulation — energy transformation, aerobic/anaerobic comparison, mechanism questions
🔑
SLP vs. Ox Phos distinction: Substrate Level Phosphorylation = ATP made directly from a phosphorylated substrate (steps 7 & 10 glycolysis; succinyl-CoA synthetase TCA). Oxidative Phosphorylation = ATP made by ATP synthase using the ETC proton gradient. Know which steps are which — this appears on almost every biochem exam.

Glycogen Breakdown & Synthesis — Mechanisms 2 hrs · Ch 21

  • 🃏 Quizlet: Glycogen enzyme deck — all enzymes, roles, bond types
  • Breakdown: Glycogen Phosphorylase cleaves α-1,4 bonds at non-reducing ends; mechanism uses PLP (pyridoxal phosphate) as acid-base catalyst; inorganic Pi attacks C1 → carbocation transition state → releases Glucose-1-Phosphate
  • Debranching: Transferase moves 3 residues to main chain (re-forms α-1,4 bond); α-1,6-Glucosidase hydrolyzes single branch residue → free glucose (NOT phosphorylated — this glucose escapes directly)
  • Phosphoglucomutase: G1P ↔ G6P; ping-pong mechanism — active site has phosphorylated Ser that first donates its phosphate to C6 of substrate, then accepts it back from C1
  • Glucose-6-Phosphatase: liver only (embedded in ER membrane); hydrolyzes G6P → free glucose + Pi; released to bloodstream; muscle lacks this enzyme so cannot export glucose
  • Synthesis: Glucose → G6P (hexokinase) → G1P (phosphoglucomutase) → UDP-glucose + PPi (UDP-glucose pyrophosphorylase; PPi hydrolyzed → 2Pi, making reaction irreversible) → Glycogen Synthase adds to non-reducing end
  • Glycogenin primer: autocatalytic Tyr-OH provides first attachment site; essential to initiate a new glycogen granule from scratch
  • Branching enzyme: breaks α-1,4 bond ~7 residues from a branch point; transfers segment to form new α-1,6 branch — increases non-reducing ends & solubility
  • Energetic advantage: glucose released as G1P (already phosphorylated) → no ATP needed to enter glycolysis (vs. free glucose requiring 1 ATP at hexokinase)
  • Tissue differences: Muscle G6P → glycolysis for local ATP. Liver G6P → Glucose-6-Phosphatase → free glucose → blood glucose maintenance
  • 📖 Workbook Self-Test Ch 21 problems 1–28 + Textbook problems 1–9, 12, 16: mechanism-focused questions without notes

Glycogen Regulation — Full Hormonal Cascade & Reciprocal Control 3 hrs · Ch 21

  • 🃏 Quizlet: Cascade & reciprocal regulation deck — PKA, PP1, phosphorylase a/b
  • Two phosphorylase forms: Phosphorylase a (phosphorylated, R-state, ACTIVE) vs. Phosphorylase b (dephosphorylated, T-state, inactive)
  • Allosteric effectors: high [AMP] shifts T→R activating phosphorylase b (muscle energy-depleted); high [ATP] = negative effector; liver phosphorylase a directly inhibited by high [glucose] (glucose is the allosteric inhibitor)
  • Muscle — Epinephrine cascade: Epi → 7TM β-adrenergic receptor → G-protein (GDP→GTP activation) → adenylate cyclase → ↑cAMP → PKA activated (cAMP binds R subunits → releases active C subunits) → phosphorylase kinase (+P, active) → phosphorylase b → phosphorylase a → breakdown ↑
  • Liver cascade route 1: Glucagon → 7TM β-adrenergic receptor → same cAMP → PKA pathway as above (glucagon = fasting hormone)
  • Liver cascade route 2 (Epi only): Epi → 7TM α-adrenergic receptor → phosphoinositide cascade → IP₃ → Ca²⁺ release from ER → Ca²⁺ binds calmodulin subunit of phosphorylase kinase → activates phosphorylase kinase → phosphorylase a
  • Turning OFF breakdown — 3 mechanisms: (1) G-protein inherent GTPase → GDP → adenylate cyclase off; (2) Phosphodiesterase degrades cAMP → AMP; (3) PP1 dephosphorylates phosphorylase kinase (−P, inactive) AND phosphorylase a → b (inactive)
  • PKA also phosphorylates small inhibitory proteins (+P) → bind PP1 catalytic site → inhibit PP1 → prevents premature shutdown while hormone signal is active
  • Insulin effect: activates glycogen synthase kinase → activates glycogen synthase → synthesis ON (fed-state signal)
  • Reciprocal regulation via PP1 (master synthesis-ON / breakdown-OFF switch): PP1 dephosphorylates phosphorylase kinase (−P, off) + phosphorylase a → b (breakdown off) + glycogen synthase b → synthase a (synthesis on)
  • PKA = breakdown ON / synthesis OFF: activates phosphorylase kinase (+P); deactivates glycogen synthase (+P); phosphorylates PP1 inhibitory proteins → inhibits PP1 (triple-locks breakdown ON)
  • Master this reciprocal table:
    SignalPKA active?PhosphorylaseGlycogen SynthasePP1Result
    Glucagon / Epinephrine✅ YESa (phospho, active)b (phospho, INACTIVE)InhibitedBreakdown ↑, Synthesis ↓
    Insulin / Fed state❌ NOb (dephospho, inactive)a (dephospho, ACTIVE)ActiveSynthesis ↑, Breakdown ↓

⚠️
Critical distinction: Glycogen Synthase is ACTIVE when dephosphorylated — the opposite of glycogen phosphorylase. This trips up almost everyone. Phosphorylation by PKA = synthesis OFF + breakdown ON. Dephosphorylation by PP1 = synthesis ON + breakdown OFF.

Pentose Phosphate Pathway — Phases, Mechanisms & All 4 Scenarios 3 hrs · Ch 20

  • 🃏 Quizlet: PPP deck — oxidative phase steps, enzyme mechanisms, all 4 cellular need scenarios
  • Two products: NADPH (reductive biosynthesis, antioxidant defense) + Ribose-5-P (nucleotides, nucleic acids)
  • Oxidative Phase (irreversible — know well): G6P + 2 NADP⁺ → Ribulose-5-P + CO₂ + 2 NADPH; 3 reactions: G6PD → 6-phosphogluconolactone → lactonase → 6-phosphogluconate → 6PG-dehydrogenase → Ribulose-5-P + CO₂
  • Rate-limiting enzyme = G6PD (Glucose-6-Phosphate Dehydrogenase); activated by high [NADP⁺] — when NADPH is depleted, pathway runs faster
  • Non-oxidative Phase (reversible): Interconverts 3, 4, 5, 6, 7-carbon sugar phosphates; outputs F6P and GAP — both glycolysis intermediates allowing bidirectional flow
  • Transketolase mechanism: TPP (Thiamine Pyrophosphate) cofactor stabilizes carbanion intermediate after 2-carbon unit is cleaved from donor; transfers 2-C unit to acceptor sugar
  • Transaldolase mechanism: Schiff base formed between active-site Lys ε-amino group and keto group of substrate stabilizes carbanion; transfers 3-C unit (dihydroxyacetone) to acceptor sugar
  • 4 cellular need scenarios — master these for exam:
    • (1) Need more Ribose-5-P than NADPH: Non-oxidative phase runs in reverse using F6P + GAP from glycolysis → transketolase/transaldolase → R5P. Oxidative phase does not run significantly.
    • (2) Balanced need for both R5P and NADPH: Oxidative phase runs; R5P is used directly for nucleotide synthesis; F6P/GAP from non-oxidative phase are recycled into glycolysis or used as needed.
    • (3) Need more NADPH than R5P: Oxidative phase runs fully; excess R5P enters non-oxidative phase → converted to F6P + GAP → enters glycolysis for ATP. Maximum NADPH production.
    • (4) Need NADPH AND ATP: Same as scenario 3 — NADPH from oxidative phase + F6P/GAP enter glycolysis → ATP. Both needs met in one coordinated flow.
  • Glutathione antioxidant system: NADPH (from G6PD) → Glutathione Reductase reduces GSSG → 2 GSH → Glutathione Peroxidase uses GSH to detoxify H₂O₂ → GSSG + H₂O
  • G6PD deficiency: cannot make NADPH → cannot regenerate GSH → RBCs accumulate oxidative damage from H₂O₂ → drug-induced hemolytic anemia (with primaquine, dapsone, etc.); X-linked recessive
  • 📖 Workbook Self-Test Ch 20 problems 11–13, 15–18, 20 + Textbook problems 7, 9: all 4 coordination scenarios without notes
